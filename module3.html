Recognizing and Avoiding Scams




Welcome to the Recognizing and Avoiding Scams learning lesson! By the end, you'll have a good understanding of commonly used scams, how to spot fake websites, and what to do if you think you've fallen for a scam.

3/6



What are online scams?


Online scams are tricks or deceptions that scammers use to steal money, personal information, or both. Scammers often pretend to be someone trustworthy, like a bank, government, or company employee with the goal of tricking you into giving them money or private information. These scams happen all over, such as in emails, through text messages, on social media, and on websites.






4/6



Common types of scams



1. Phishing scams: Phishing is when scammers send fake emails or messages that look like they come from a trusted organization, such as your bank or a government agency. They try to trick you into clicking on links that ask for personal information, like your credit card number or Social Security number.
How to spot a phishing email:
-Look for unusual or missspelled words
-check the email address carefully
-Be suspicious of urgent messages such as "update your account" or "act quickly"
-Don't click on links or open attachments unless you're sure the message is legitimate

2. Lottery or prize Scams: You may receive an email or message telling you that you’ve won a prize, but in order to claim it, you have to pay a fee or provide personal information. Legitimate organizations never ask you to pay upfront to claim a prize.
How to avoid these scams:
-If you didn't enter a lottery or contest, you didn't win
-Be cautious about sharing any payment or banking information online

3. Tech support scams: Scammers may call you, claiming to be from a tech company like Microsoft or Apple. They say there’s a problem with your computer and ask for access to fix it. They may even ask for payment to resolve the issue.
How to protect yourself:
-Never give remote access to anyone you don't trust
-If you receive an unsolicited call, hang up and contact the company directly

4. Online dating scams: Online dating scams involve criminals who create fake profiles on dating websites or apps to trick individuals into sending them money, gifts, or personal information. These scammers often target older adults who may be looking for companionship, affection, or a romantic relationship. They pretend to be interested in the victim, build a relationship over time, and then make requests for money or help in some way.
How to avoid online dating scams:
-Never send money or gifts to someone you haven't met in person
-Look for red flags in their profile, such as reluctance to meet in person
-Don't share personal information too soon





5/6



Red flags and warning signs of scams



1. Unsolicited Messages: If you receive an unexpected message or phone call, be cautious. Scammers often contact people out of the blue to try to get them to act quickly without thinking.
2. Requests for personal information: Legitimate companies and government agencies won’t ask for sensitive information (like your Social Security number or bank details) through email, text, or over the phone. If someone asks for this kind of information, it’s a red flag.
3. Too good to be true offers: If something sounds too good to be true – like winning a huge amount of money or receiving a free gift – it probably is! Scammers often offer fake rewards to lure people in.
4. Pressure tactics: Scammers may try to make you feel rushed or pressured, saying things like, “You must act now!” or “This offer expires today!” Don’t let anyone pressure you into making quick decisions.








6/6



What to do if you think you've fallen for a scam




1. Stop all communication: If you’ve been contacted by a scammer, immediately stop responding to any calls, emails, or messages.
2. Report the scam: You can report scams to organizations like the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), the Better Business Bureau (BBB), or your local authorities. This helps prevent others from falling victim to the same scam.
3. Protect your accounts: If you shared sensitive information, such as your credit card or bank details, contact your bank or credit card company right away to report the fraud. They may be able to help you protect your accounts.
4. Monitor your accounts: Keep an eye on your financial accounts for any suspicious activity. If you see anything unusual, report it immediately.
